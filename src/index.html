Raju

This elderly man is named Raju. He has his name tattooed on his hand. Raju makes a living for his family by driving a rickshaw in the heart of Yangon. At the age of 56, Raju resides in Dala, a town across the river from Yangon.
Have you noticed the expression on Raju’s face, reflecting a system’s decay? Only if you desire to see it will you truly perceive it. It is possible that you might not empathize with this because you have not experienced it yourself.
Raju’s left eye is clouded over, yet his right eye still has the opportunity to witness the complexities and beauty of Yangon. Successive regimes have systematically oppressed the masses. The oppressive rulers have methodically destroyed the education system, indoctrinating the populace with distorted ideologies, and thus abusing education. Since the coup in 2021, the already struggling working class has sunk deeper into the mire. Ultimately, the people from the working class have become like frogs trapped in a well.
Look closely at the smile on Raju’s face. It carries a faint smile mocking the decaying system. People like Raju endure much hardship in Myanmar.

U Phone Myint

U Phone Myint founded a small library named "Pyithu Alin" in one of Yangon’s townships. This haven he created became a sanctuary for elderly grandparents, serving as a cherished retreat. The elderly found solace there, surrounded by books, journals, and love, and it became their second home where they could communicate with each other. This little haven alleviated their loneliness and thrived for nearly a decade. However, following the military coup in 2021, their second home was destroyed. Now, this haven lives on in the heart of an eighty-year-old grandfather. No gun can ever extinguish our feelings.

Kan Kaung

The dog's name is Kan Kaung. He was abandoned in a garbage dump in Dala Township, on the outskirts of Yangon, during a rainstorm. When he was abandoned, three of his legs were broken. He was rescued and adopted from that garbage dump by a woman who sells snacks for a living. Her compassion gave Kan Kaung a chance to survive.
The woman travels from Dala to Yangon daily, carrying Kan Kaung in her bicycle basket, to the place where she sells snacks. Kan Kaung is always by her side. Because Kan Kaung is a disabled dog, the woman takes care of his needs, including those related to his injuries.
She belongs to the working-class, living day by day, selling snacks on platforms in Yangon. Her status and love for Kan Kaung are not materialistic.
Their bond has lasted for nearly four years. In this world, there are still forms of love that cannot be bought with money or power.

Ye Ye

The settlement near the Botahtaung waterfront is a fragile home for homeless families. One family, living amidst the bustling Botahtaung waterfront, makes ends meet primarily by selling bird feed and fish. The children from these families often collect beer cans and liquor bottles. Some of them ask for money from people drinking alcohol near the waterfront. A few of the children sell bird feed. Among these children, some have dropped out of school due to the family's need to survive.
They couldn't achieve a bright future because they didn't reach the turning point in their education. They faced life's challenges in ways beyond their age. Despite being so young, they managed to take care of their basic needs through their own efforts. After the coup in 2021, everything became more difficult for the lower classes. Rey Rey is a child who hasn't been to school yet. When I first met Rey Rey, he wasn't wearing shoes. The next time we met, his grandmother had bought him a pair of shoes because he had managed to get more money the previous day. A basic necessity that a child should easily have, he had to struggle to obtain. This is a comedy from a world you wouldn't want to see.

DaLa

He is an ordinary person who drives a ferry between Yangon and Dala (Thamada Beach). He is the head of his household and has a naturally chubby build. He has accepted this as an unchangeable aspect of his appearance. Dala is a township where many of Yangon's working-class people live. After the military seized power, the resulting economic downturn directly impacted his family. The ferries, which use diesel, have become more difficult to operate due to the rising fuel costs. The meager income from driving the ferry is just enough to feed his family of five for the day. His daily earnings are only sufficient for their daily meals.
The root of all these problems is the oppressive system. The real culprit is the authoritarian regime. Now, his family is worried about being unjustly conscripted into the military, following the illegal conscription law issued by the military government on February 10. While wealthier people can bribe officials to avoid conscription, this issue has reached the doorsteps of working-class families in the city.
